Introduction

Convolution is a mathematical operation on two functions that produces a third function expressing how the shape of one is modified by the other. It is a cornerstone concept in engineering, particularly in signal processing and systems theory.

In this post, we will explore the key properties that make convolution powerful and the theorems that link time-domain operations to the frequency domain.

Section 1: Mathematical Definition

The continuous convolution of two functions $x(t)$ and $h(t)$ is defined as:

$$ (x * h)(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(\tau)h(t-\tau) d\tau $$

For discrete signals, the summation replaces the integral:

$$ (x * h)[n] = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} x[k]h[n-k] $$ [!NOTE] The symbol $*$ denotes convolution, not multiplication. Context is key when interpreting equations.

Section 2: Fundamental Properties

Convolution obeys several algebraic properties that simplify system analysis.

1. Commutative Property

The order of convolution does not matter:

$$ x(t) * h(t) = h(t) * x(t) $$

This implies that filtering a signal $x$ with impulse response $h$ yields the same result as filtering $h$ with $x$.

2. Associative Property

When convolving three functions, the grouping does not affect the result:

$$ (x * h) * g = x * (h * g) $$

This is crucial when analyzing cascaded systems.

3. Distributive Property

Convolution distributes over addition:

$$ x * (h + g) = (x * h) + (x * g) $$

This allows parallel systems to be combined into a single equivalent impulse response.

[!TIP] Use the Associative property to simplify block diagrams of cascaded LTI systems by combining impulse responses before performing calculations.

Section 3: Convolution with Special Functions

Certain functions act as identities or shifters during convolution.

Identity Element

Convolving any function with the Dirac delta function $\delta(t)$ returns the original function:

$$ x(t) * \delta(t) = x(t) $$

Shifted Delta

Convolving with a shifted delta function results in a time shift:

$$ x(t) * \delta(t - t_0) = x(t - t_0) $$

Section 4: The Convolution Theorem

One of the most powerful tools in engineering is the Convolution Theorem, which links convolution in the time domain to multiplication in the frequency domain.

Fourier Transform Context

If $X(f)$ and $H(f)$ are the Fourier Transforms of $x(t)$ and $h(t)$ respectively, then:

$$ \mathcal{F}\{x(t) * h(t)\} = X(f) \cdot H(f) $$

Conversely, multiplication in time corresponds to convolution in frequency:

$$ \mathcal{F}\{x(t) \cdot h(t)\} = X(f) * H(f) $$ [!WARNING] Ensure you are using the correct transform pair (Fourier, Laplace, or Z-transform) as scaling factors may differ depending on the definition used.

Conclusion

Understanding convolution properties allows engineers to manipulate systems algebraically rather than solving complex integrals every time. The Convolution Theorem, in particular, bridges the gap between time and frequency analysis, enabling efficient filter design and signal manipulation.
