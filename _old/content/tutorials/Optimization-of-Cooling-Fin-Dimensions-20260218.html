Original Exam Questions

Q1

Optimization of Cooling Fin Dimensions

A cooling fin for a machine is designed to have a rectangular cross-section. If the fin’s base dimensions are $x$ and $y$ (in cm) and the heat conduction capacity $h(x,y) = xy$, use the method of **Lagrange Multipliers** to minimize the surface area of the fin, $S(x,y) = 2xy + 20y + 20x$, while maintaining the heat conduction capacity $h(x,y) = 400\text{cm}^2$.

Detailed Answer & Step-by-Step Logic

Solution for Question 1 Calculus III: Constrained Optimization

To minimize the surface area $S(x,y) = 2xy + 20y + 20x$ subject to the constraint $h(x,y) = xy = 400$, we apply the following mathematical workflow:

1
Define Functions

Objective: $f(x,y) = 2xy + 20y + 20x$

Constraint: $g(x,y) = xy - 400 = 0$

2
Formulate the Lagrangian

Introduce the multiplier $\lambda$ to create the auxiliary function:

$L(x,y,\lambda) = (2xy + 20y + 20x) - \lambda(xy - 400)$
3
Partial Derivatives

Set all partial derivatives to zero to find stationary points:

$\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = 2y + 20 - \lambda y = 0$
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial y} = 2x + 20 - \lambda x = 0$
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda} = -xy + 400 = 0$
4
Solve the System
  • From $L_\lambda$: $xy = 400$
  • Isolating $\lambda$ from $L_x$: $\lambda = \frac{2y + 20}{y} = 2 + \frac{20}{y}$
  • Isolating $\lambda$ from $L_y$: $\lambda = \frac{2x + 20}{x} = 2 + \frac{20}{x}$
  • Equating both: $2 + \frac{20}{y} = 2 + \frac{20}{x} \implies \mathbf{x = y}$
  • Substitute into constraint: $x^2 = 400 \implies \mathbf{x = 20, y = 20}$ (ignoring negative lengths).
5
Final Evaluation

Substitute $x=20$ and $y=20$ back into the surface area equation:

$S(20, 20) = 1600 \text{ cm}^2$

The optimal dimensions are a square cross-section of 20cm x 20cm.

Fundamental Method

The Lagrange Multiplier Workflow

This technique identifies local extrema of a function $f$ subject to equality constraints $g = c$ by finding where the gradients of both functions are parallel.

Step 1: Setup

Define your target function $f$ and your constraint $g(x,y) = c$.

Step 2: The Gradient Equation

Solve $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$. This is the core of the method.

Step 3: Solve & Check

Solve the system with the constraint and check your physical domain limits.

Pro Tips for Success
  • Check for division by zero when isolating $\lambda$.
  • Verify symmetry—it often simplifies the algebra.
  • Ensure dimensions $x, y > 0$ in physical problems.
